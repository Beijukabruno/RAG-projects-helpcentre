# What is cancer
Cancer is a family of diseases that results when body cells change and grow in an abnormal and uncontrolled way. 
These abnormal cells accumulate and form swellings (also known as tumors). The abnormal cells can also spread from one part of the body to another ,a process called metastasis.
Cancer can start in any part of the body. The signs and symptoms of cancer depend on which body part the cancer starts in, and to which body parts the cancer has spread.
Cancer is not witchcraft or a curse, and cancer is treatable, and it can be cured if it is detected early and appropriated treatment is obtained.

The abnormal growth in cancer cells occurs due to damages in the DNA. DNA is the substance in body cells that carries genes or hereditary information such as a person’s height or skin color, or whether a man grows a bald head or not. Because DNA determines how the body grows and functions, damage to DNA results into problems such as cancer. There is no single known cause of cancer, but there are many conditions, called risk factors, that increase the chances of developing cancer. 

# Signs and symptoms
The signs and symptoms of cancer vary depending on the type of cancer and which body parts the cancer has spread to or how advanced the cancer is. Generally the more advanced the cancer is, the more severe the signs. In early stage, cancer may not have any signs or symptoms, or they may not be very obvious. The common signs and symptoms of cancer include:
swellings e.g. of breast, abdomen, neck, jaw, or lymph nodes,
Pain of the body part affected by cancer
bleeding e.g. from the private parts in cancer of the cervix, or from the gums or nose in cancers of the blood
difficulty passing urine e.g. in cancer of the prostate, 
easy breakage of bones e.g. in bone cancer,
difficulty in eating or swallowing (for example if the cancer is in the mouth or throat)
skin changes e.g. in skin cancer or Kaposi’s sarcoma
Other signs and symptoms such as weight loss, fever, fatigue and anemia are also common and can occur in any cancer.
It is important to remember that having these signs or symptoms does not automatically mean that a person has cancer since they can also be signs or symptoms of other illness. For example, fever and anemia are common in malaria, and many swellings are non-cancerous (which is called benign tumor).
However, if you have these symptoms and signs and they last for long e.g. 2 to 4 weeks, or if they rapidly get worse, please see a qualified health worker to check if it is cancer or not, and to get the appropriate treatment.

# Risk factors
Cancer risk factors are conditions or things that increase the chances of developing cancer. Having a risk factor does not automatically mean that the person will develop the cancer, but also a person can develop cancer without any obvious risk factors. Also, different cancers have different risk factors. Cancer risk factors include:
Sex: Some cancers are more common in one sex than in the other. For example, cancer of the breast is 100 times more common in females than in males. Obviously, some cancers are strictly for one sex because the other sex lacks the organ. Men do not have a cervix, so they can’t develop cervical cancer, and women don’t have a prostate so they can’t develop prostate cancer.
Age: Generally the older one gets, the higher the chances of developing cancer. However, some cancers such as Wilm’s tumor (a cancer of the kidneys) or Retinoblastoma (a tumor of the eyes) are almost exclusively tumors of childhood.
Family history and genetics: If your biological parent or sibling had cancer, your chances of developing cancer are higher. This is because cancer occurs due to damage to DNA (or the genes) and these damages can be passed on from parents to children. If a non-biological relative, such as a husband or wife or step children, has cancer, this does not affect your chances of developing cancer. Cancer is not contagious and cannot be passed on from one person to another through bodily contact such as sexual intercourse 
Smoking and alcohol abuse: Smoking is a risk factor for very many cancers, but especially cancer of the lungs, mouth, throat, uterus, pancreas, and blood cancer. There is no safe amount of smoking, and even second-hand smoking (that is, non-smokers being exposed to smoke from others people who smoke), can be harmful. Excess consumption of alcohol is also a risk for cancer especially cancer of the liver.
Infections: Many viruses are involved in the causation of cancers, for example, human papilloma virus (or HPV) is responsible for almost all cervical cancers, and Hepatitis viruses are involved in liver cancer causation. HIV is also involved in the causation of several different cancers including cancer of the cervix, Kaposi’s sarcoma, blood cancers, rectal cancer, etc. A bacteria called H. Pyroli which causes stomach ulcers is involved in causation of stomach cancer, and a parasitic flatworm that causes bilharzia is involved in causation of bladder cancer.  The good news is, many of these infections can be prevented e.g. through vaccination against HPV and Hepatitis virus, using condoms to prevent infections, and proper treatment for HIV or H pyroli
Other risk factors for cancer include poor diet and being overweight or obese, lack of physical activity, exposure to chemicals such as pesticides used in farming, or asbestos (which was found in older roofing materials) or exposure to a lot of radiation.

# Cancer types
There are hundreds of different cancers, which are usually named after the body part that the cancer starts in. Some of the commonest cancers in Uganda are
Cervical cancer which starts in the cervix (or the mouth of the womb). This is the number one cancer, but it affects only women (since men don’t have a cervix)
Breast cancer, which starts in the breast. Breast cancer can occur in men too, but it is much more common in women.
Prostate cancer, which originates in the prostate gland. The prostate is found in the male reproductive system, so prostate cancer occurs only in men.
Kaposi’s sarcoma, which occurs on the skin and the lining of the mouth, intestines or lungs
Lymphoma which occurs in the lymph tissues such as lymph nodes
Leukemia which is blood cancer,
Lung cancer
Throat cancer
Liver cancer
Intestinal cancer, etc

# Cancer in children
Although cancer is more common in adults, children also do get cancer. Moreover, cancers in children tend to grow more rapidly than in adults and can quickly lead to complications or death within a few weeks.
 The cause of cancer in children is not well known. In adults, there are well known cancer risk factors such as infections, smoking or other environmental toxins which cumulatively damage the body as one grows older and thus lead to cancer. Childhood cancers usually do not have such clear risk factors. A small percentage of cancers in children are due to a damage in the DNA that is inherited from the parents.
The good news is that cancers in children are generally more curable than in adults, but this of course depends on the type and stage of the cancer, and on whether proper treatment is obtained. The commonest cancers in children are blood cancers (known as leukemia), cancers of the lymphomas (known as lymphomas), cancer of the kidney (called Wilm’s tumor), and cancer of the eyes called Retinoblastoma.
Screening ,diagnosis and staging
Cancer screening aims at finding cancer at an early stage, before a person has any signs and symptoms. Screening is important because by the time signs and symptoms appear, cancer may have begun to spread and be harder to treat.
When a person has symptoms, or if cancer is suspected during screening, then diagnostic tests must be done to confirm whether it is really cancer or not. And if cancer is confirmed, the stage of the cancer must be determined since treatment depends on the stage.
Screening
# During screening, the following are done:
Physical examination in which a health worker checks the body by looking or feeling for lumps or swellings, skin changes, or any other  signs of illness. The health worker will also ask you about past illness or health habits such as physical exercise or diet which are important cancer risk factors.
Laboratory tests in which samples of blood, urine, or body cells are taken and examined for evidence of cancer or pre-cancer lesions, cancer risk factors (e.g. infections), or other signs of illness
Imaging tests such as ultra sound scan, endoscopy, mammography and other X-ray examinations, to find any swellings inside the body.
Always remember that screening does not confirm cancer, and more tests are needed if an abnormality is found during screening. Sometimes screening tests are positive but on further testing cancer is not found. This is called false positive, and can cause unnecessary worrying. On the other hand, screening tests are negative when actually the cancer is there. This is called false negative, and it leads to delay in finding the cancer. It is therefore important to go for regular screening even if you were found negative in a prior screening.
In some cancers, for example cancer of the cervix, screening can detect pre-cancer lesions. Pre cancer lesions are changes that occur in the body cells which if left untreated lead to cancer. In many other cancers, there are no pre cancer lesions, and screening helps to detect the cancer early.

# Diagnosis
Cancer treatment cannot be given unless the cancer has been confirmed because different cancers are treated differently. Often the only way to confirm whether a person has cancer or not, is through a biopsy. Biopsy means taking off a small piece of the suspicious swelling or bone marrow in case of blood cancers, and examining this sample in a special laboratory by specialist doctors called pathologists. In some cancers, confirmation of the diagnosis can be made using a combination of laboratory and imaging tests such as CT scan, MRI or endoscopy. 
There are many myths and misconceptions about biopsies which make people scared. For example, people often worry that a biopsy will make the cancer grow and spread faster. This is not true.
Depending on the location of the suspicious swelling, the process of taking off a biopsy can be simple and quick, for example if the swelling is on the skin, or it might require a surgical operation in theatre, for example if the cancer is in the intestines.
Sometimes there might be complications such as pain or bleeding from a biopsy site, but these can be minimized by pain medication and proper preparation such as doing blood checks to ensure the patient doesn’t have higher than normal risk of bleeding. The doctor will assess you and advise if complications are anticipated.

# Cancer staging
Besides the biopsy for confirming the cancer, doctors do other test including lab and imaging tests, so as to understand the stage of the cancer and whether the patient is strong enough for the treatment. Cancer stage means how advanced the cancer is, and what parts of the body have been affected. There are 4 stages: stage 1 is the earliest stage with only small size of cancer that is limited to the body part where it started, while stage 4 is the most advanced stage in which cancer size is big, and the cancer has spread from the body part of origin to other parts. For example if cancer of the breast is found as a small lump in one breast, it is stage 1, but if it is a big swelling and the cancer spreads to the lungs or bones then it is stage 4. Cancer in stage 3 or 4 is difficult to treat, so it is important to go for cancer screening and treatment in time to reduce chances of having these late stages.



# Cancer treatment
There are many types of cancer treatment. These include chemotherapy (or chemo in short), radiotherapy, surgery, and palliative care. The choice of treatment depends on many factors including, for example, the type of cancer, cancer stage, availability of treatment and the condition of the patient. A patient can be given one or more types of cancer treatments, and these can be given at the same time, or one after another. Generally cancer treatment takes a long time, at least 3 to 6 months, although this can be longer depending on the patient’s condition and how well he or she responds to treatment. However, most of the treatment is done while the patient comes from home if he or she is strong enough and doesn’t require admission.
It is important to remember that a big part of care and treatment for cancer patients is not for treating the cancer itself, but rather it is supportive care for treating the complications of cancer, the side effects of cancer treatment and for treating other diseases that the cancer patient might have. # chemotherapy (Chemo)
Chemo involves use of drugs in form of pills, injections or drips. Typically several drugs are given in combination, and in repeating phases called cycles. On average, 6 cycles are given, and these are spaced 2 to 4 weeks apart to allow the body to rest and recover from the side effects of each cycle. Variation in duration, schedule and spacing of cycles, and the number of cycles are common depending on how well the patient responds to treatment and the side effects. This variation should not make you worried, and always speak to your doctor or nurse if you have any concerns.

# Surgery
Cancer surgery involves cutting out the cancer swelling or the body part that is affected by cancer. Surgery is most suitable for patients with early stage cancer where the whole cancer tumor can be cut out. Surgery can be completed in one day, but often many days or weeks are needed to prepare for operation, including counseling of the patient and family, getting the surgical team needed, doing some tests such as X-rays or blood checks, or just waiting for the patient to gain strength if he or she is very ill and weak. 

# Radiotherapy
Radiotherapy involves use of radiation energy to destroy cancer cells. Some people describe radiotherapy as burning, or describe the radiation energy as electricity. This is can be misleading. The radiation energy is similar to the energy used for making X-ray images, but in higher doses. You will not see or feel anything during radiotherapy. Radiotherapy is also most beneficial when the cancer is early stage and limited to one body part because it is easier to target the radiation energy to the cancer swelling. Radiotherapy is given in phases called fractions. Depending on the type of radiotherapy machine, a fraction can last just minutes or an hour, but usually one fraction is given per day. The number of fractions, and therefore the number of days, depends on the type of cancer and patient condition.

# Palliative care
Palliative care focuses on controlling symptoms and making the patient and his/her family comfortable. Pain control is one of the most important goals of palliative care, but also psychological, spiritual and social support. Therefore, all patients need palliative care, even in the early stages, but it increasingly becomes more important in late stages of cancer where the symptoms are more severe and chances of cure are less. In terminal patients, i.e. those with incurable cancers and are very ill, palliative care might be the only care given to ensure a comfortable dying because giving other treatments such as surgery and chemo would have less value compared to the side effects and cost. However, sometimes small amounts of chemo or radiotherapy can be given during palliative care if they can help with better control of symptoms such as pain. Surgery can also be done, for example to remove a large swelling that might be painful, infected, smelling or heavy and limiting the patient’s movement, breathing or eating.

# Side effects
Cancer treatment, like any other treatments, can have side effects. Side effects are problems that occur when the treatment affects normal body parts. Some cancer treatments cause more side effects than others, some side effects occur in one cancer treatment and not in another, and different patients might get different side effects from the same treatment. Most side effects are temporary, and will go away after the treatment is over. The following are some of the side effects of cancer treatment
Nausea and Vomiting
Sores or small wounds in the mouth and throat
Diarrhea or constipation
Anemia, i.e. not having enough blood
Bleeding and bruising
Infections
Loss of hair
Fatigue
Loss of appetite
Problems with memory and concentration, i.e. forgetfulness and inability to focus
Nerve problems, i.e. feeling of numbness or pins-and-needles
Skin and nail changes or discoloration
Problems with sleep

Some side effects, e.g. infections, bleeding, and anemia, are more harmful and can result into death compared to others like hair loss or nerve problems. Because of this, doctors and nurses might care more about the more harmful side effects and tend to ignore the others.
Therefore, you should always ask your doctor or nurse about the side effects of the treatment you are going to receive, and speak up about the side effects or any other problems that you might experience during cancer treatment. Most of the side effects can be treated or reduced with nutrition, hygiene or life style changes, or with medication e.g. medication for vomiting, sleeping pills, etc.

# What to expect during cancer care at the UCI
Every cancer patient’s journey and experience is different. This section provides you a general overview of cancer care at the Uganda Cancer Institute

# About the Uganda cancer Institute (UCI)
The UCI is a public facility under the ministry of health. The UCI  has been providing cancer treatment and research for over 50 years. It has all necessary medicines, equipment and experts needed for managing cancer in Uganda. The UCI is located in Kampala, on Mulago hill, but also has satellite clinics at Mbarara hospital and in Mayuge, and more sites are being set up.
The UCI is open daily and offers cancer screening, diagnosis, treatment, counseling and psychosocial support, as well as cancer research and training. The UCI also runs outreaches to different parts on the country where cancer screening and awareness are done.

# Referral process
You can come to the UCI even if you are not a cancer patient, to get cancer awareness and education, or for cancer screening. These services are offered daily, and free of charge. Most people, however, come to the UCI as referrals from other health facilities after being confirmed or suspected to have cancer.
If you are suspected or confirmed to have cancer and referred to the UCI, you should promptly come since delaying allows time for the cancer to advance to late stage.
Beware of herbalist or faith healers who might confuse you and make you delay. These alternative healers cannot appropriately treat cancer.
Please remember to bring from the referring health facility all the medical documents such as lab results, scans or X-ray images, HIV treatment book for those who are HIV positive, prescription and medication for chronic conditions such as diabetes or high blood pressure, etc.
For patients already undergoing treatment at the UCI, a return date is given upon discharge depending on the schedule of the treatment. However, if you have an emergency before your scheduled date, please immediately return to the UCI. The emergency clinic is open daily to attend to all those with emergencies or new patients.


# Cost of treatment
Services at the Uganda Cancer Institute are free of charge, including consultation, cancer awareness and screening, admission, blood transfusion, medication, and lab tests.. The only exception is imaging tests such as X-ray or scans which are paid for. There is also a private wing where services are paid for. . In any case the costs are indicated on notice boards at the UCI, and payments are done at the cashier’s office, and an official UCI receipt is issued.If anyone asks you for money for services at the UCI other than those mentioned, report to the UCI reception at the Rose Mary Nankabirwa shade or ask the UCI staff for advice. The staffs have uniforms with their names or identification cards clearly visible.
Please note that you will incur personal costs for example in transportation to and from hospital, airtime, food, or for buying a drug that might be out of stock. These costs can be significant since cancer treatment takes a relatively long time. Moreover, because you might not be able to work during cancer treatment, your income might be affected. It is therefore important to seek for support from family members or friends if you are not financially stable. 
There are social workers at the Uganda Cancer Institute whom you should discuss with concerning social and financial challenges. In some cases arrangements can be made to reduce costs, for example in transportation by getting you a hostel to stay near the Uganda Cancer Institute in between the treatment cycles, or by transferring the patient to a branch of the Uganda Cancer Institute e.g. in Mbarara. Sometimes the type or schedule of treatment can also be based on socio-economic reasons.
