## FOREWORD 

###### Cervical cancer is one of the most common cancers among women worldwide. In Uganda, where 

###### screening services have not been widely available coupled with the limited awareness, cervical 

###### cancer has become a huge public health problem. Cervical cancer is the number one incident 

###### cancer among women in Uganda followed by breast cancer. According to recent evidence from 

###### GLOBOCAN IACR 2012, the age standardised incidence and mortality rates for cervical cancer are 

###### 44.4/100,000 and 27.4/100,000 respectively. 

###### It is now apparent that cervical cancer, which is caused by the Human Papilloma Virus, can be 

###### prevented through vaccination and early screening to detect pre-cancerous lesions that can be 

###### treated. However, we find that majority of the women (over 80%) are diagnosed at the advanced 

###### stage of the disease when survival is minimal. Estimates have shown that approximately 3915 

###### women are diagnosed with cervical cancer each year and about 2275 die from the disease. These 

###### figures will keep on rising if nothing is done in terms of prevention and early treatment and hence 

###### this will consequently result into decrease in productivity and economic development. 

###### The goal of the National Cervical Cancer Strategic plan is to reduce the incidence, prevalence and 

###### mortality from cervical cancer among Ugandan women for the period 2018 – 2023. This strategy 

###### has been developed in line with the Uganda National Health policy 2010 and the Uganda Health 

###### Sector Strategic and Investment Plan (2010/11-2019/20) which have included cancers among

###### UNEPI comprehensive Multi-Year Plan (cMYP 2012-2016), draft National Cancer Control Strategy 

###### and Plan of the Uganda Cancer Institute. Therefore, this calls for collective and multi-stakeholder 

###### efforts to prevent, control and manage cervical cancer using cost effective measures. 

###### The Government of Uganda appreciates and acknowledges the different stakeholders who 

###### convened to put together this strategy. The leadership from the Uganda Cancer Institute together 

###### with the Ministry of Health is highly appreciated. This strategy therefore seeks to prioritise and 

###### advocate for cervical cancer prevention, control, treatment and palliative care initiatives.

## 1.0 INTRODUCTION 

#### 1.1 Defining Cervical Cancer 

##### Cervical cancer is the uncontrolled growth of cells on the cervix or opening of the womb 

##### caused by the Human Papilloma Virus (HPV). This virus causes a common infection of the 

##### reproductive tract and is transmitted sexually. The peak time for infection is shortly after 

##### becoming sexually active and the first sexual contact is often deemed the first point of 

##### exposure to HPV. Persistent infection with the virus results in changes which, if not 

##### identified through screening, diagnosis and treatment, may develop into pre-cancer and 

##### cancer. 

##### Signs and symptoms of cervical cancer 

##### Signs and symptoms of cervical cancer usually appear in later stages of the disease and 

##### may include: 

- Inter-menstrual bleeding. 

- Abnormal contact vaginal bleeding that may appear before or after sexual 

##### intercourse. 

- Unexplained pain either in the back, pelvic area or legs. 

- Fatigue, weight loss, loss of appetite. 

- Vaginal discomfort 

- malodorous discharge. 

- Groin lymph node enlargement

##### The public health goal is to decrease the number of deaths due to cervical cancer. 

##### Interventions include: 

- A referral linkage and followup mechanism from primary care providers to 

##### facilities that offer cancer diagnosis and treatment; 

- Accurate and timely cancer diagnosis, by exploring the extent of invasion

- Palliative care: Effective treatment and palliative care in tertiary facilities 

##### and at the community level, including access to opioids.

##### control programme include performance and impact indicators.

- _Screening rate of the target population_ : Percentage of women aged 25–49 years

##### 1.2.4 Why the Cervical Cancer burden is High 

##### In many developing countries, access to health services is limited and screening for 

##### cervical cancer either is non-existent or reaches few of the women who need it. In these 

##### areas, cervical cancer is the most common cancer in women and the leading cause of 

##### cancer death among women (WHO)^6. 

(^6) Cervical cancer screening and treatment in Uganda. Carolyn Nakisige, Melissa Schwartz, and Anthony Okoth Ndira. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gore.2017.01.009

##### The primary underlying cause of cervical cancer is infection with one or more high-risk types 

##### of the human papillomavirus (HPV), a common virus that is sexually transmitted. Most new 

##### HPV infections resolve spontaneously; if it persists, infection may lead to the development of 

##### precancer which, left untreated, can lead to cancer. As it usually takes 10–20 years for 

##### precursor lesions caused by HPV to develop into invasive cancer, cervical cancers can be 

##### prevented by early detection and treatment of precancerous lesions. 

##### The main reasons for the higher incidence and mortality in developing countries are: 

- Lack of awareness of cervical cancer prevalence and risk factors among the 

##### population, health care providers and policy-makers; 

- Low coverage of HPV vaccination programs; 

- Absence or poor quality of screening programmes for pre-cancer lesions and early- 

##### stage cancer. In women who have never been screened, cancer tends to be 

##### diagnosed in its later stages, when it is less easily treatable; 

- Limited access to health care services for prevention, treatment and management; 

- Lack of functional referral systems.

##### 2.2.3.3 Tertiary care for invasive cervical cancer 

##### Optimal surgical management for early-stage cervical cancer requires expertise in 

##### radical curative surgery, well equiped facilities with a high level of intra/ post

##### operative care and availability of adjuvant chemoradiotherapy. Although the 

##### health care delivery system provides general surgical services at general, regional 

##### referral and national referral hospital levels, the capacity for surgical care for 

##### cervical cancer is limited due to a shortage of skilled or trained gynacological 

##### oncology surgeons. Currently, only 8 of the regional referral hospitals are offering 

##### diagnostic services and none offers surgical services for cervical cancer. 

##### Women with suspected cancer at the various screening sites are referred to 

##### tertiary hospitals for colposcopy where available, cervical biopsy (histopathological 

##### diagnosis) and staging of the cancers. Appropriately staged women undergo 

##### surgery depending on the stage. Most women that would benefit from radical 

##### surgery are undergoing simple hysterectomies due to lack of expertise in radical 

##### surgery, and the majority of these women have recurrence of cancer due the sub

##### optimal diagnosis and subsequent management. 

##### Currently there is one fuctional cobalt radiotherapy machine in Uganda at the UCI 

##### in Kampala possing a challenge of accessiblity for many women.

#### 2.7 Rationale for the Strategic Plan 

##### Recognising that Uganda is one of the high burden countries of cervical cancer in terms of 

##### incidence and mortality in the world currently^17 , and knowing that evidence based cervical 

##### cancer prevention and treatment technologies are widely available including a 10 year 

##### experience with provision of VIA and cryotherapy, it is imperative that the Ministry of 

##### Health and partners consolidate the gains and best practices learnt so far and also 

##### galvanise in country efforts to address the situation. 

##### Uganda implemented a cervical cancer strategic plan, mainly through stakeholders 

##### and development partners. Several pilots have been done however, none has moved

## 3.0 STRATEGY FOR IMPLEMENTATION 

#### 3.1 Strategy for implementation 

##### The implementation of the strategic plan will utlise a multi-sectoral approach with 

##### emphasis on galvanising efforts and core competencies of stakeholders currently 

##### working in cervical cancer prevention. Some of these stakeholders include WHO, 

##### UNFPA, MoH, districts, PATH, UNICEF, the EPI program, hospitals as well as district 

##### health management committees. The HPV TWG committee will be expanded to reflect 

##### comprehensive cervical cancer prevention and control needs. 

##### The MoH will provide leadership of the program, assess program needs and address the 

##### major gaps. The major strategies will follow the WHO recommendations as shown 

##### below; 

**_Figure 3:_** _WHO Recommendations. *For Uganda's context, Screening of women starts at 25 years and above_ 

##### There is overwhelming evidence for human papillomavirus (HPV) testing as the most 

##### effective method for primary cervical-cancer screening. HPV testing is more sensitive 

##### and reliable for detection of precancer, cervical intraepithelial grade 3 (CIN3) or 

##### adenocarcinoma in situ (AIS), and CxCa than Pap testing. The increased sensitivity of 

##### HPV testing over Pap testing cervical precancer and cancer translates into two 

##### important health benefits. First, earlier detection of CIN3/AIS lesions by HPV testing 

##### that if treated results in a reduced incidence of CxCa within 4-5 years and-related death 

##### within 8 years compared to Pap testing and VIA. Second, greater reassurance against 

##### CxCa following a negative result for many years, which permits safe extension of 

##### screening intervals. HPV testing has also been shown to be much more effective than 


<!-- PAGE_BREAK -->
##### VIA in reducing CxCa-related death within 8 years. Indeed, where resources permit, the 

##### World Health Organization (WHO) now recommends HPV testing for cervical cancer 

##### screening of women aged 30-49 years. New resource-stratified guidelines for cervical 

##### cancer screening by the American Society of Clinical Oncology recommend that HPV 

##### testing should be the primary screening modality at all resource levels. 

##### HPV testing-based cervical cancer screening has been piloted in clinical studies and has 

##### been shown to be effective and cost-effective in Uganda. The next steps are to begin 

##### implementation in high-risk populations who might glean the greatest benefit. In this 

##### strategic plan, Uganda will integrate and scale up community-based, health system 

##### delivery of HPV testing-based cervical cancer screening into the existing VIA based 

##### screening. This will be at regional referral and general hospitals and health centre IVs 

##### with access to GeneXpert machines and/or CareHPV machines. These facilities will be 

##### supported to increase on their capacity to implement cervical cancer screening and 

##### management of screen-positive women. These facilities will be obliged to carry out out

##### reaches in the hard to reach areas where the careHPV test will be most appropriate in 

##### mass outreaches. 

##### Women will be tested for HIV (if HIV has not already been assessed), TB, and HPV using 

##### GeneXpert (Cepheid, Sunnyvale, CA, USA), a multi-analyte, random-accesss, clinical 

##### laboratory platform that can deliver HPV testing in one hour without interrupting 

##### clinical testing for other analytes (i.e., HPV, HIV, and TB testing for the same woman 

##### concurrently). GeneXpert is widely available in Uganda, making scale-up feasible, and 

##### currently available GeneXpert systems have sufficient capacity to offer HPV testing to 

##### high-risk women. 

##### Women who test HPV positive will undergo a triage VIA test then proceed to either 

##### ablative treatment with thermocoagulation or cryotherapy if the are eligible. Those 

##### whose lesions are not eligible will be referred for LEEP or surgical evaluation and 

##### management or palliative care. 

##### Treatment of eligible pre-cancer will be done on same visit or referral for Cryotherapy 

##### or thermocoagulation. LEEP eligible women will receive treatment at Regional Referral 

##### Hospitals and surgery as appropriate. UCI will provide radiation treatment while 

##### palliative care will be made available at hospitals.
